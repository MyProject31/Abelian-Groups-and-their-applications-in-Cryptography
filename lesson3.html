Lesson 3

Modular Arithmetic

Congruences

Definition

Let \(n\) be a non-zero natural number. We define an equivalence relation on \(\mathbb{Z}\) as follows:

For all \(a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\), we say that \(a\) is congruent to \(b\) modulo \(n\), and write

\[ a \equiv b \pmod{n} \]

if and only if

\[ n \mid (a - b) \]

i.e., \(a - b\) is divisible by \(n\).

Examples

  • \(17 \equiv 5 \pmod{12}\) because \(17 - 5 = 12\), divisible by 12.
  • \(23 \equiv 2 \pmod{7}\) because \(23 - 2 = 21\), divisible by 7.

Properties

Let \(a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}\) and \(n\) a non-zero natural number:

  1. Reflexive: \(a \equiv a \pmod{n}\)
  2. Symmetric: If \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\), then \(b \equiv a \pmod{n}\)
  3. Transitive: If \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\) and \(b \equiv c \pmod{n}\), then \(a \equiv c \pmod{n}\)
  4. Compatibility with addition: If \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\) and \(c \equiv d \pmod{n}\), then \(a+c \equiv b+d \pmod{n}\)
  5. Compatibility with multiplication: If \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\) and \(c \equiv d \pmod{n}\), then \(ac \equiv bd \pmod{n}\)
  6. If \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\) and \(a \equiv b \pmod{m}\), \(n\) and \(m\) coprime, then \(a \equiv b \pmod{nm}\)
  7. If \(ac \equiv bc \pmod{n}\) and \(\gcd(c,n) = 1\), then \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\)

Modular Multiplicative Inverse

Definition

A modular multiplicative inverse of an integer \(a\) is an integer \(b\) such that the product \(ab\) is congruent to 1 modulo \(n\):

\[ ab \equiv 1 \pmod{n} \]

Remark

A modular inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\) exists if and only if \(a\) and \(n\) are coprime.

Properties of Modular Inverse

  1. \(a\) has an inverse modulo \(n\) if and only if \(\gcd(a,n) = 1\).
  2. Modular inverse is unique: \(ab \equiv 1 \pmod{n} \implies b\) is unique mod \(n\).
  3. If \(b\) is the inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\), then \(a \equiv b^{-1} \pmod{n}\) and \(b \equiv a^{-1} \pmod{n}\).
  4. If \(a\) and \(b\) both have inverses modulo \(n\), then \((ab)^{-1} \equiv a^{-1}b^{-1} \pmod{n}\).
  5. The inverse of 1 is always 1: \(1^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}\).
  6. The inverse of a negative number \(a\) is minus the inverse: \((-a)^{-1} \equiv -a^{-1} \pmod{n}\).
  7. If \(n\) is prime and \(a\) has an inverse, \((a^k)^{-1} \equiv (a^{-1})^k \pmod{n}\).

Modular Exponentiation

Definition

Modular exponentiation is the process of repeatedly squaring a number and reducing it modulo some integer, then combining the results to find the final value. Given integers \(a\), \(n\), and \(m\), it computes:

\[ a^n \mod m \]

Example

Compute \(3^{13} \mod 7\):

  • \(3^1 \equiv 3 \mod 7\)
  • \(3^2 \equiv 9 \equiv 2 \mod 7\)
  • \(3^4 \equiv (3^2)^2 \equiv 2^2 \equiv 4 \mod 7\)
  • \(3^8 \equiv (3^4)^2 \equiv 4^2 \equiv 16 \equiv 2 \mod 7\)
  • Combine powers: \(3^{13} \equiv 3^8 \cdot 3^4 \cdot 3^1 \equiv 2 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 \equiv 24 \equiv 3 \mod 7\)

Therefore, \(3^{13} \mod 7 = 3\).

Euler's Function

Definition

Euler's function \(\phi(n)\) is the number of positive integers less than or equal to \(n\) that are coprime to \(n\):

\[ \phi(n) = |\{ k \in \mathbb{N}^* \mid 1 \le k \le n \text{ and } \gcd(k,n) = 1 \}| \]

Examples

  • \(\phi(1) = 1\)
  • \(\phi(6) = 2\)
  • \(\phi(9) = 6\)

Properties

  1. Prime number: If \(p\) is prime, \(\phi(p) = p-1\)
  2. Multiplicative: If \(\gcd(m,n)=1\), then \(\phi(mn) = \phi(m)\phi(n)
  3. Prime power: If \(p\) prime, \(\phi(p^k) = p^k - p^{k-1} = p^k(1 - 1/p)\)
  4. General formula: For \(n = p_1^{a_1} \cdots p_r^{a_r}\), \(\phi(n) = n\prod_{i=1}^r (1-1/p_i)\)

Example: \(n = 12 = 2^2 \cdot 3\), then \(\phi(12) = 12(1-1/2)(1-1/3)=4\)

Euler's Theorem

If \(\gcd(a,n)=1\), then

\[ a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n} \]

Examples:

  • \(a=3, n=10\): \(3^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{10}\)
  • \(a=2, n=9\): \(2^6 \equiv 1 \pmod{9}\)

Fermat's Little Theorem

If \(p\) is prime and \(p \nmid a\), then

\[ a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p} \]

Equivalent form:

\[ a^p \equiv a \pmod{p} \]

Examples:

  • \(a=2, p=7\): \(2^6 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}\)
  • \(a=5, p=11\): \(5^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}\)
